What is BYEE?

Across Asia, youths are responding to environmental crises through an expanding ecosystem of grassroots initiatives. From founding organisations, running campaigns, and demanding accountability from governments and corporations. Yet the distribution of this activity is far from even. Certain issues, geographies, and communities consistently attract youth-led attention, while others remain largely invisible. These patterns are not random, for they reflect structural inequalities in who has the resources, freedoms, and platforms to organise, and whose environments are treated as worthy of concern. This project, BYEE (Blindspots in Youth Environmental Efforts), is a systematic attempt to map that uneven landscape. Drawing on original research across countries and territories in Asia as defined by the UN Geoscheme, it constructs a public database of youth-led environmental initiatives, categorised by geography, thematic focus, organisational type, and scope. The goal is not simply to catalogue what exists, but to surface what is absent: the communities underserved by youth environmental action, the environmental issues that receive disproportionately little attention, and the structural conditions that produce these gaps. The concept of a "blindspot" is central to this project's framing. A blindspot is not merely an absence, but one that carries normative weight. Preliminary findings suggest that youth environmental activity in Asia clusters heavily in capital cities, in wealthier and more politically open countries, and around issues that are already well-represented in global environmental discourse. Meanwhile, countries affected by conflict or authoritarian governance, communities in rural and remote regions, and environmental challenges with lower media visibility such as freshwater ecosystem degradation, glacial retreat, or sand extraction remain consistently underrepresented. This report situates these empirical findings within a framework of environmental justice. Drawing on John Rawls' Difference Principle and Veil of Ignorance, and extended through the work of Brian Barry and Thomas Pogge to international and intergenerational contexts, it asks: if we did not know where in Asia we would be born, or to which generation we would belong, what distribution of environmental attention and action would we consider just? The blindspots this project identifies are, in Rawlsian terms, a departure from what a fair distribution of environmental concern would look like — and understanding them is a prerequisite for addressing them.

Inclusion Criteria

An initiative qualifies if it meets all of the following:

Youth-led
Founded by and currently led by people aged 15–30 (standard UN definition). The initiative must still be primarily driven by under-30s today, not just at founding.
Environmentally focused
Activities directly address at least one of SDG 12, 13, 14, or 15. SDG tags are assigned by the researcher based on documented activities — not self-reporting.
Based in or operating within Asia
Countries defined by the Statistics Division of the United Nations Secretariat (UN Geoscheme).
Demonstrably active
Evidence of activity within the past 6 months, sustained with proof of action via online presence at least once every 3 months. Many projects start — and nothing ever happens. This criterion filters them out.
Independent
Not a youth wing of an adult-run NGO, not state-orchestrated. For social enterprises: environmental impact must be the primary purpose, not a byproduct of a commercial business.
Why These SDGs?

BYE uses four SDGs as its thematic framework — chosen because they directly concern the state of the natural world. Here is why each matters, and why their neglect carries consequences.

SDG 14 — Life Below Water

Pollution (plastic and runoff), overfishing, coral bleaching, and coastal ecosystem destruction are all exacerbated by unsustainable consumption and population growth. Two-thirds of marine areas have been damaged by human activity, and a third of sharks and rays and a third of reef corals are threatened with extinction. Tackling the loss of life under water requires addressing not just what we put into the ocean, but the systems driving it — including family planning, women's education and empowerment, which can enable more women to participate in marine resources management and mitigate the impacts of climate change.

SDG 15 — Life on Land

According to WWF, we have lost 60% of all vertebrate wildlife populations since 1970 — a period in which the human population more than doubled. A landmark 2019 UN assessment explicitly noted that population growth is an indirect driver of biodiversity loss and stated: "changes to the direct drivers of nature deterioration cannot be achieved without transformative change that simultaneously addresses the indirect drivers." Conservation efforts must incorporate population solutions to be truly effective long-term.

SDG 12 — Responsible Consumption & Production

Material footprint per capita in high-income countries is 60% higher than in upper-middle-income countries and more than 13 times the level of low-income countries. We are already using resources 1.75 times faster than they can regenerate — and unless things change, we will require three Earths to supply our needs by 2050. Responsible consumption is not a lifestyle choice. It is a structural challenge requiring systemic response.

SDG 13 — Climate Action

Every additional person on our planet adds more emissions. A comprehensive review by Project Drawdown found that slowing population growth through educating girls and providing family planning would be one of the most powerful ways to reduce atmospheric CO₂ by 2050. The 2019 Scientists' Warning of a Climate Emergency, endorsed by more than 11,000 scientists, called for ending and ultimately reversing human population growth among other transformative actions to avert the worst effects of climate change.

SDG context adapted from Population Matters (populationmatters.org/un-sdgs), Project Drawdown, and the 2019 Scientists' Warning of a Climate Emergency.
